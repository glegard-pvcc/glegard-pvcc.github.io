What was Obergefell v. Hodges?

Obergefell v. Hodges was a Supreme Court case in regards to same-sex marriage and the Fourteenth Amendment. James Obergefell and petitioners argued to Richard Hodges of the Ohio Department of Health
that denying them their right of marriage violated their Fourteenth Amendment rights.


The Journey of the Case

Before this case, 13 states had bans on same-sex marriage. James Obergefell and 15 other petitioners from same-sex couples, from the states of Ohio, Tennessee, Michigan, and Kentucky, all filed suits to their states
Federal District Court claiming they were having their constitutional rights violated by preventing them from marrying who they wish. They claimed that the states 1) would not allowed them to marry someone of the same
sex in their own state and this violated the Due Process and Equal Protection Clauses in the Fourteenth Amendment and 2) they wouldnot recognize marriages performed in states where same-sex marriages were protected,
also violating their constitutional rights. In all four cases of the four states mentioned, the trial courts agreed with the plaintiffs. When it went to the U.S. Court of Appeals, it was reversed, and they said the marriage refusal was
not in violation of their constitutional rights and that it was a states issue. Then, the issue went to the Supreme Court, with Justices Roberts, Scalia, Kennedy, Thomas, Ginsburg, Breyer, Alito, Sotomayor, and Kagan.

Result

The Supreme Court came to a 5-4 ruling of the plaintiffs, saying that the Fourteenth Amendment protects and guarantees all their right to marry no matter the case. During the ruling, Justices Scalia, Thomas, Alito, and Chief Justice
Roberts voted that the prevention of marriage was not a violation of constitutional rights, and Justices Ginsburg, Breyer, Sotomayor, and Kagan voted in favor of the plaintiffs, with Justice Kennedy breaking the tie, saying their Due
Process and Equal Protection was violated. Chief Justice Roberts then wrote a dissent saying that same-sex marriage could be fair and beneficial, but that it is not protected by the Consitution. He says it should not be up to his court
to decide and is at the liberties of the States themselves. Justice Scalia joined in the dissent saying that those in the majority rule overstepped the Supreme Court's power and were acting in a legislative manner rather than a judicial
one. Justice Thomas also joined the dissent saying that the consensus distorted the democratic process and stretched due process and that they were taking power from the legislature by infringing on religious freedom.
