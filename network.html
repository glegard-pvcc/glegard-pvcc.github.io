Network Terminology
TERM DEFINITION
network classifications
    LAN: local area network, consists of a collection of computers in a single building or building complex
    MAN: metropolitan area network: network of intermediate size, such as one spanning a local community
    WAN: wide area network, links machines over a greater distance—perhaps in neighboring cities or on opposite sides of the world
network models
    bus network: machines are all connected to a common communication line called a bus
    star network: one machine serves as a central focal point to which all the others are connected
protocols
    CSMA/CD ethernet: dictates that each message be broadcast to all the machines on the bus
    CSMA/CD wifi: wireless networks adopt the policy of trying to avoid collisions rather than trying to detect them
combining networks
    repeater: a device that passes signals back and forth between the two original buses
    bridge: a device that passes signals back and forth between the two original buses
    switch: a bridge with multiple connections, allowing it to connect several buses rather than just two
    router: connection between networks to form an internet is handled by devices known as routers, which are special purpose computers used for forwarding messages
    gateway: point at which one network is linked to an internet is often called a gateway because it serves as a passageway between the network and the outside world
client/server model
    P2P: peer-to-peer, involves processes that provide service to and receive service from each other
    distributed system: consist of software units that execute as processes on different computers
    cloud computing: huge pools of shared computers on the network can be allocated for use by clients as needed
internet addressing
    tier 1: consist of very high-speed, high-capacity, international WANs. These networks are thought of as the backbone of the Internet. They are typically operated by large companies that are in the communications business
    tier 2: more regional in scope and less potent in their capabilities
    tier 3: Tier-1 and tier-2 ISPs are essentially networks of routers that collectively provide the Internet’s communication infrastructure. The many possible pathways between routers gives this core of the Internet redundancy, meaning that most or all of the network remains connected even when very important tier-1 routers fail. Access to this core is usually provided by an intermediary called an access or tier-3 ISP
IP address
    ICANN: Internet Corporation for Assigned Names and Numbers, which is a nonprofit corporation established to coordinate the Internet’s operation
    DNS: name servers are used as an Internet-wide directory system known as the domain name system
    TLD: these suffixes are called top-level domains, used to reflect the domain’s classification
    country-code TLDs: new two-letter TLDs for each specific country
internet protocols
    FTP: File Transfer Protocol, application for accessing another computer from a great distance used the Telnet protocol
    HTTP: Hyper Text Transfer Protocol, webservers and browsers have become more sophisticated, more and more of these traditional network applications have come to be handled by webpages
    SMTP: Simple Mail Transfer Protocol, a way that two computers on the network may interact when transmitting an email message from one host to the other
world wide web
    URL: each document is given a unique address called a Uniform Resource Locator, contains the information needed by a browser to contact the proper server and request the desired document
    client-side activities: they are performed by a client (such as a browser), what you see on screen
    server-side activities: they are performed by a server (such as a webserver), what happens in the background for your screen to appear that way
internet four-layer software
    application layer: consists of those software units such as clients and servers that use Internet communication to carry out their tasks
    transport layer: accept messages from the application layer and to ensure that the messages are properly formatted for transmission over the Internet
    network layer: decide in which direction a packet should be sent at each step along the packet’s path through the Internet
    link layer: responsibility of transferring the packet
internet is:
    packet-switching network: digital data is sent by breaking the data into blocks of bits called packets, which contain both the data being transmitted and control information for routing the data
    fault tolerant: continue operating properly in the event of the failure of one or more faults within some of its components
    redundant: increases the reliability of the Internet and helps it scale to more devices and more people
cybersecurity
    malware: malicious software, might be transferred to, and executed on, the computer itself, or it might attack the computer from a distance
    virus: software that infects a computer by inserting itself into programs that already reside in the machine
    worm: autonomous program that transfers itself through a network, taking up residence in computers and forwarding copies of itself to other computers
    trojan horse: program that enters a computer system disguised as a desirable program, such as a game or useful utility package, that is willingly imported by the victim
    spyware: software that collects information about activities at the computer on which it resides and reports that information back to the instigator of the attack
    phishing: a technique of obtaining information explicitly by simply asking for it. The term phishing is a play on the word fishing because the process involved is to cast numerous “lines” in hopes that someone will “take the bait.”
    DDoS attack: Distributed Denial of Service, work of generating the attacks is distributed over many hosts
protection
    firewall: primary prevention technique is to filter traffic passing through a point in the network
    spam filter: firewalls designed to block unwanted email
    proxy server: a software unit that acts as an intermediary between a client and a server with the goal of shielding the client from adverse actions of the server
    antivirus software: used to detect and remove the presence of known viruses and other infections
    cryptography: study of sending and receiving messages securely in the presence of adversaries
    public key encryption: involves techniques by which the encryption and decryption algorithms are known, and one of the keys is known, but the encoded message remains secure
